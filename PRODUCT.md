# Product

## Register

split

## Users

Tabletop gamers coordinating sessions: D&D / RPG campaign groups (4-6 players), Magic: The Gathering Commander pods, and board game crews. Organizers are typically the one person in the group who reluctantly becomes the logistics lead. Players are their friends who are bad at checking group chats. Both audiences are comfortable with technology but not interested in learning new tools. Sessions usually happen in evenings and weekends, often planned a week or two out. The app is used in casual, couch-or-desk contexts.

## Product Purpose

TabletopTime ends the "when are we playing?" group chat spiral. It finds the overlapping availability window for a gaming group, respects quorum (you can play with 4 of 6), and removes all friction for the people who just need to vote. No logins, no apps to install, no data mining. Available both as a free cloud-hosted service and as a self-hostable Docker image for privacy-minded users.

## Brand Personality

Warm, nerdy, direct. The tone of a friend who runs a friendly local game store (FLGS): knowledgeable, enthusiastic about the hobby, low-pressure. Never corporate. Never trying to upsell. The product feels handmade by someone who actually plays games, not built by a product team optimizing for engagement.

Three-word summary: **welcoming, precise, honest**

## Anti-references

- **Enterprise SaaS** (Notion, Monday.com, Asana): cold hierarchy, subscription-first, feature bloat, success metrics everywhere
- **Generic schedulers** (Doodle, When2Meet): no gaming identity, dated visual language, treats every use case identically
- **Social media patterns**: feeds, engagement loops, notification bait, follower counts, public performance of activity

## Design Principles

1. **The party before the product.** Players are the point. Friction is the final boss. Every screen should feel like it's standing aside to let the game happen.
2. **Trust is built by what we don't collect.** Privacy-first and open source aren't footnotes. The design should feel like it means it: no dark patterns, no fake urgency, nothing that feels extractive.
3. **Tool precision, gamer warmth.** The scheduling logic is rigorous. The personality is human. Precision and warmth are not opposites.
4. **Made for groups, not enterprises.** This is for six people trying to play D&D on a Saturday. Intimacy is a feature, not a limitation. Never signal scale.
5. **Dark for a reason.** Late-night sessions, dim living rooms, monitors in basement game rooms. Dark is the natural habitat. Make it feel earned and considered, not default.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA compliance. Keyboard navigable, screen-reader compatible. Sufficient contrast on dark backgrounds. Motion-safe defaults: no autoplay animations that can't be paused or are disorienting for users with vestibular disorders.
